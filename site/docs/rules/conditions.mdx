# Conditions

Some attacks, traps, spells, or other effects can inflict conditions—usually negative effects other than damage.

| Condition | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| <RuleRef id="blinded" /> | Can't see. Attacks against you have advantage; your attacks have disadvantage. |
| <RuleRef id="charmed" /> | Sees the charmer as an ally. Charmer has advantage on social interactions with you. |
| <RuleRef id="dazed" /> | Heroes lose 1 action; monsters perform one less action on their next turn. |
| <RuleRef id="frightened" /> | Disadvantage on rolls when source of fear is nearby; speed halved when moving closer to it. |
| <RuleRef id="grappled" /> | Cannot move. Attacks against you have advantage. |
| <RuleRef id="incapacitated" /> | Can't do anything. Attacks against you have advantage; melee attacks that hit, crit. |
| <RuleRef id="invisible" /> | Cannot be seen. Your attacks have advantage; attacks against you have disadvantage. |
| <RuleRef id="poisoned" /> | Disadvantage on rolls. |
| <RuleRef id="prone" /> | Movement costs twice as much; disadvantage on attacks. Melee attacks against you have advantage. |

## Minor Statuses

Some effects apply **minor statuses** — <RuleRef id="smoldering" />, <RuleRef id="charged" label="Charged" />, and <RuleRef id="distracted" label="Distracted" /> are examples. These statuses **do nothing on their own** and automatically **end when combat ends**. However, certain spells and abilities have additional effects when triggered against targets bearing a specific minor status.

> **Example:** *Smoldering* does nothing by itself — but the Fire spell *Ignite* deals massive bonus damage to a Smoldering target and ends the condition on hit.
